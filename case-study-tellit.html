Tellit: Rebuilding Trust Between Startups and Investors

An investor relations platform that transforms fundraising from scattered chaos into organized clarity. By replacing email black holes with beautiful, trackable investor pages, Tellit gives founders the visibility and confidence they need to close funding rounds.

The Problem

Fundraising in the Dark

"I sent our deck to 40 VCs. Got 3 responses. No idea if the other 37 even opened it."

— Early-stage founder during user research

Every founder I interviewed described the same nightmare. You spend weeks perfecting your pitch deck, craft the perfect cold email, hit send to 50 VCs, and then... nothing. This opacity isn't just frustrating—it's strategically dangerous.

Founders make critical decisions based on guesswork: Should I follow up? Did they even see it? Is there interest or am I wasting my time? I started this project by asking: What if founders could see exactly what investors do with their materials?

Research Insights

Five Core Pain Points

Through interviews with 12 early-stage founders, five critical problems emerged. What's at stake? Founders are losing funding opportunities because they can't identify warm leads. They're wasting precious runway on investors who'll never engage. And they're walking into pitch meetings unprepared, without knowing what concerns they need to address.

This wasn't just an information problem—it was a trust problem.

01

Complete Opacity

Zero visibility into investor engagement. Founders have no idea if their materials were viewed, shared, or immediately deleted.

02

Scattered Materials

Pitch decks, one-pagers, financials, and team bios scattered across email threads, Dropbox links, and Google Drives.

03

No Access Control

Once you email a PDF, you lose all control. No way to update materials or revoke access from investors who passed.

04

Missing Context

No insight into which questions matter most to investors or what concerns are preventing them from moving forward.

05

Impossible Follow-ups

Without engagement data, founders can't follow up intelligently. Every outreach feels like a shot in the dark.

The Solution

One Link. Full Visibility. Living Pages.

Instead of emailing PDFs into a void, founders create beautiful investor pages that consolidate everything an investor needs in one shareable link. The key insight: Founders don't need another document management tool. They need a platform that treats investor relations as an ongoing relationship, not a one-time email blast.

Every design decision stemmed from three core principles:

One Link for All Context

Instead of juggling multiple files and links, founders share a single URL that contains pitch deck, team info, Q&A, and supporting materials. Investors get everything they need without hunting through email threads.

Full Visibility into Engagement

Track every page view, file download, and question opened. Know exactly which investors are interested and what concerns matter most to them.

Living Pages That Evolve

Update materials in real-time without resending links. Fix typos, add new metrics, answer emerging questions—all without breaking existing investor access.

Feature Breakdown

Hero Section Design

The hero section had to immediately convey professionalism while maintaining approachability. I designed eight customizable themes that let founders express different brand personalities—from minimal and sophisticated to bold and energetic.

The customization panel gives instant visual feedback as founders adjust colors and themes. But notice the constraints: I'm not giving unlimited customization, because too much freedom creates ugly pages. Instead, I designed eight pre-balanced themes and let founders choose colors within guardrails that ensure sufficient contrast.

This approach gives founders ownership over their visual identity while preventing truly bad design decisions.

Building Trust

Team & Value Proposition Sections

Investors invest in people, not just ideas. I designed the team section as a clean grid of headshots with minimal decoration—no fancy animations that would distract from the faces themselves.

The value proposition section uses a two-column layout with generous paragraph space and that signature magenta accent bar you see throughout the system. That electric pink serves as the platform's energy source—in a category dominated by blues, I wanted Tellit to feel distinctly different. Magenta conveys creativity, confidence, and innovation.

But I paired it with restraint. The near-black text and generous whitespace create breathing room, preventing the accent from overwhelming.

Team section with clean grid layout and professional photography

Preemptive Answers

The Strategic Q&A Section

This Q&A section was one of the most important design challenges. Instead of waiting for investors to email questions—which creates friction and delays—founders can preemptively answer the questions they know investors will ask.

I designed a clean accordion pattern with smooth expansion animations and that blue accent bar showing the active question. The analytics dashboard tracks which questions get opened most frequently, giving founders data-driven insight into what concerns matter to their target investors.

Actionable Insights

Analytics Dashboard

This was the hardest design challenge. How do you visualize complex engagement data in a way that's instantly understandable to busy founders?

I started by listing every metric we could track—20+ potential data points. Then I ruthlessly prioritized based on one question: What decisions do founders need to make?

The final dashboard focuses on four core insights: Who's interested? What's working? What questions matter? How are my files performing?

Complete analytics dashboard with New Leads, Leading Firms, Leading Q&A, and File Downloads widgets

Notice the "Complete Your Tellit" progress widget at the top. This gamifies the setup process—showing founders what's incomplete and gently guiding them toward best practices. It's one of my favorite subtle touches because it serves triple duty: gamification, reminder, and guidance—all without being preachy.

Each widget is independently scannable—you get the core insight in 3 seconds, but can dig deeper if needed.

Foundation

The Design System Behind the Screens

From day one, I knew this needed a proper design system. Tellit isn't just one page—it's marketing site, investor pages, editor views, analytics dashboards, and settings panels.

I built a token-based system where every color, spacing value, and typography size references a central source of truth. The accent color appears consistently across CTAs, section dividers, and interactive elements. The spacing system uses a 4px base unit with Fibonacci-inspired scaling that creates natural rhythm.

This system added two weeks to the timeline, but it saved hundreds of hours downstream. When we added new features, everything felt visually cohesive without requiring new design work. The engineering team could implement screens without constantly asking clarifying questions.

01

Color Tokens

Complete palette with semantic naming: primary, secondary, accent, success, warning, error. Each with dark/light variants for accessibility.

02

Typography Scale

Modular scale from 12px to 64px with consistent line heights and letter spacing. Separate scales for display, heading, and body text.

03

Spacing System

4px base unit creating rhythm: 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128px. Applied consistently to padding, margins, and gaps.

04

Component Library

45+ reusable components with documented props, states, and variations. Each component designed for composition and flexibility.

Design Process

Three Major Iterations

The investor page architecture went through three major iterations. Sometimes the answer isn't more clever information architecture—it's less. The sectioned scroll is technically the simplest solution, but it took three iterations to realize simple was right.

01

Everything Visible

First attempt: Show everything at once on a single long-scroll page. Problem: It was overwhelming. Investors didn't know where to start. Everything competed for attention equally.

02

Tab-Based Sections

Second attempt: Tab-based sections—Overview, Pitch Deck, Team, Q&A. Problem: Content felt hidden. Investors couldn't see the full picture without clicking through multiple tabs.

03

Sectioned Scroll

The breakthrough: Sectioned scroll with progressive disclosure. The hero grabs attention, then value proposition sets context, then natural flow down the page. Solved discoverability AND analytics.

Iteration 1: Everything visible (overwhelming)

Iteration 3: Sectioned scroll (final solution)

Critical Feedback

The Access Control Pivot

"I don't want to micromanage access per file. I just want to know: Should this investor have access or not?"

— Early access founder, Week 3 testing

One of the most valuable pieces of feedback came from an early access founder. Our initial design had separate permission levels for decks, one-pagers, and other materials—very granular control.

That single piece of feedback led us to simplify to global access control per lead. One toggle: access or no access. The entire access management system became 3x simpler, and founders actually used it instead of ignoring it because it was too complex.

This taught me: Sometimes founders don't want more control. They want less cognitive load.

Before: Granular Permissions

Separate access controls for pitch deck, one-pager, financials, team bios, and supporting documents. Required 5+ decisions per investor. Founders found it overwhelming and often just gave everyone full access.

After: Simple Toggle

One switch per investor: access or no access. Simple, fast, actually used. Completion rate for setting permissions jumped from 23% to 89%.

Reflections

Five Critical Lessons

01

Design Systems Are Worth It

The upfront investment in a comprehensive design token system paid dividends throughout the project. New features felt cohesive without additional design work.

02

Founders Need Confidence

The design work that mattered most wasn't fancy animations—it was the care and polish that made founders feel professional and prepared.

03

Data Without Action Is Noise

Early dashboard designs tried to show everything we tracked. It was overwhelming. The breakthrough came from asking "What decision should this data drive?"

04

Accessibility Makes Design Better

Designing with constraints from day one didn't limit creativity—it forced better decisions. High contrast text is easier for everyone to read.

05

Mobile Isn't an Afterthought

40% of investor views happen on phones. Redesigning for mobile after screens were "done" was painful. Future projects: design mobile simultaneously with desktop.

Looking Forward

What I'd Do Differently

What Worked

The design system foundation provided consistency across all features. The sectioned page architecture balanced discoverability with analytics. The actionable analytics dashboard focused on decisions, not just data.

What Could Improve

The onboarding flow needs templates, not blank canvases. File management needs more depth for supporting documents. The mobile editing experience is currently desktop-only.

What I'd do differently: Start with templates to reduce setup anxiety. Build the file manager earlier in the process. Test with actual investors, not just founders. Design email integrations from the start.

What's next: The opportunities are huge. AI-powered pitch optimization, investor network effects, integrated fundraising CRM, community-driven Q&A libraries. We're just getting started.

"The best design replaces anxiety with clarity."

— Project Reflection

Tellit taught me that the best design work often isn't visible to users at all. It's the system that makes everything consistent. The constraints that prevent bad decisions. The defaults that create beautiful outcomes without requiring expertise.

Fundraising is never easy. My goal wasn't to make it easy—my goal was to replace anxiety with clarity, guesswork with data, and scattered chaos with organized professionalism.
